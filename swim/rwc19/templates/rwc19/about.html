Rules

  1. You must be an openwater swimmer on Auckland North shore, or be associated with one. Basically if you know Jo, Alton, Jim, Geoff or Helen you are in.
  2. The game is based on 11 matches: All Black pool games (4); all quarter finals (4), semi-finals (2) and final. The aim of the game is to finish with the lowest total points score.
  3. $10 to enter the competition
  4. Predicted scores must be provided in person, via the Open Water swimming social WhatsApp group or Jim’s new website. Entries must be received a minimum of 15 minutes before kick-off.
  5. People who have paid (or pledged their $10), but forget to post their predicted score, or choose not to post a predicted score, will be assigned the average points scored by other competitors.

Scoring

  1. You must get the basic result right, pick the right winner, or choose a draw. If you don't get this right you get the worst score from those who did get it right
  2. Then the difference between your winners score and the actual winners score is calculated & divided by 2
  3. Then we that actual spread (diff between the winner & loser) and your spread, take the difference and add it
  4. If you picked the exact score of the winner you get 5 points removed

Prizes

  • A coffee and a muffin (or equivalent) will be awarded to the winner of every round.
  • The remainder of the cash will accumulate until after the final, when a first, second, third and consolation prize is awarded, for the players with the lowest, second-lowest, third-lowest and highest points scores.

Judging

  • Meegan ‘the accountant’ Brewer has reviewed the points calculations. Jim ‘don’t call me honey’ West and Jo ‘Frau’ Hammer are administrators, and nominally the judges, and their decision is final. Recourse to legal and mediation services will be limited because the billable minutes will erode all gains.
  • The winner of each round is the person who first gets the actual result correct, then whose estimated score is closest to that of the winning team, in combination with the points difference (or spread). Getting close to the winning score is twice as important as getting close to the points difference.
